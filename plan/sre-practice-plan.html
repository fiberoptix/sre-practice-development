01 — Thesis

Why Middle Office Needs a Dedicated SRE Practice

Middle Office sells electronic trade processing services to BNY's clients. Every minute of downtime, every failed trade, every missed SLA erodes client trust and revenue. Yet the team has no formal reliability discipline — incident response is reactive, monitoring is fragmented across too many tools with no unified reliability view, and changes are the #1 source of production incidents.

The core problem: Without SLOs, error budgets, or structured toil measurement, the team cannot objectively prioritize reliability work against feature delivery. The SRE who should be engineering reliability is instead firefighting the consequences of ungoverned change. This plan fixes that.

Case study — why policy without enforcement fails: A base image upgrade policy reduced container vulnerabilities by 80% in Q1–Q3 2025. Within months, compliance decayed — teams stopped upgrading to the quarterly base images. Today, 80% of Docker images are built on base images more than 6 months old, and vulnerabilities are back up. The policy was right. The enforcement mechanism was missing. This plan fixes that with automated build-breakers in the CI/CD pipeline (Tenet 4) — so compliance is enforced at build time, not by memo.

03 — The Six Tenets

SRE Tenets, Prioritized

Six pillars, ordered by dependency and impact. Each tenet has a clear "what," "how," and "why first." Click any card to expand.

1
Observability & SLOs
Months 1–5 · Foundation
Define what "reliable" means in numbers. Without SLIs/SLOs/error budgets, every other tenet lacks a compass.
click to expand →

What to Build

  • SLIs per critical service (trade processing latency, settlement success rate, message throughput)
  • SLOs co-defined with Business and Client Services
  • Error budgets tied to release governance
  • Middle Office SRE Dashboard — a dedicated reliability view that does not exist today. MyCIO shows what's broken now; the SRE Dashboard shows how reliable we are over time, how fast we're burning error budget, and whether we should ship or stabilize. This is a concrete engineering deliverable (see build options below).

How to Develop

  • Audit top-5 services by incident volume
  • Workshop with dev leads + COO to agree SLO targets
  • Instrument SLIs from existing AppDynamics/Splunk telemetry
  • Build SRE Dashboard — platform decision (Grafana, MyCIO extension, or both) to be evaluated in Phase 0
  • Publish weekly SLO burn reports

Why First

  • Gives leadership an objective reliability score — not "is anything broken right now" but "how reliable are we this month"
  • Error budgets create natural prioritization framework — no more arguing about reliability vs. features
  • MyCIO already aggregates vendor data — adding SLO/SLI panels extends what people already look at
  • Directly maps to BNY SRE JD: "defining SLOs and SLIs, improving observability"
  • Builds on existing monitoring investment
2
Incident Remediation Pipeline
Months 2–5 · Formalize
RCAs happen fast and PRBs get executive briefings — but findings die there. Build the missing bridge from "we know what happened" to "here's the engineering work that prevents it next time."
click to expand →

What to Build

  • Remediation pipeline: every PRB produces tagged action items (monitoring, alerting, QA gate, policy, code fix)
  • Action item tracking with ownership, SLO linkage, and due dates
  • Preventive controls catalog: what monitoring/alerting/QA should have caught this?
  • On-call runbooks per service (built from recurring incident patterns)
  • Monthly incident trend analysis tied to error budgets

How to Develop

  • Extend the existing RCA → PRB → exec briefing with a mandatory "remediation actions" section
  • Classify each action: new alert, monitoring gap, QA test, code fix, policy change, runbook update
  • Route actions into JIRA with SRE/Dev ownership — track completion rate quarterly
  • Build runbook repo in Confluence from the top-10 recurring incident types
  • Monthly review: which remediations shipped, which incidents would have been prevented

Why Second

  • The RCA/PRB process is already strong and fast — this tenet closes the last mile
  • Remediation actions feed directly into Tenets 3–5 (automation, CI/CD, resilience)
  • Turns executive briefings from "here's what happened" into "here's what we're doing about it"
  • Maps to JD: "leading incident management through postmortems and automated recovery"
3
Toil Elimination & Automation
Months 5–10 · Prove Value
Measure manual, repetitive work — then systematically automate it. The Google SRE rule: keep toil below 50% of an SRE's time.
click to expand →

What to Build

  • Toil taxonomy and tracking system
  • Automation for the highest-toil operational tasks (identified by measurement)
  • Self-healing automation for known failure modes
  • AI-assisted tooling for rapid development of support scripts and validation tools

How to Develop

  • Time-track all operational tasks for 2 weeks — classify as toil vs. engineering
  • Rank toil tasks by frequency × time × risk of error
  • Automate top-5 toil tasks by rank
  • Set quarterly toil reduction target (e.g., −20%)
  • Measure: are we spending less time on toil each quarter?

Why Third

  • Directly addresses the problem statement: too much time on incidents, not enough on engineering
  • Returns time — each automation frees hours for Tenets 4–6
  • Maps to JD: "automating infrastructure and operations"
  • Quick, visible wins build organizational buy-in
4
Release Engineering & CI/CD Maturity
Months 5–10 · Reduce Change Risk
Changes cause most incidents. Mature the pipeline from "builds and deploys" to "safe, observable, rollback-ready releases." Enforce base image compliance for both active and dormant containers — build-breaker + scheduled refresh.
click to expand →

What to Build

  • Base image compliance system — two-pronged: (1) build-breaker that rejects stale bases on active builds, (2) scheduled refresh pipeline that auto-rebuilds dormant containers and opens MRs or JIRAs. Covers 100% of containers.
  • Automated pre-deploy and post-deploy validation gates
  • Canary / blue-green deployment patterns
  • Change failure rate (CFR) tracking per service
  • Error-budget-aware release gating

How to Develop

  • Implement base image build-breaker and scheduled refresh pipeline (see playbook for detailed design)
  • Extend MO pipeline standard with automated smoke tests post-deploy
  • Integrate SLO dashboards into deploy pipeline — block releases when error budget exhausted
  • Pilot canary deploys on one service with automated rollback
  • Build deploy-time metric capture (before/after latency, error rate)

Why Fourth

  • Directly attacks root cause: change-related incidents and Monday P3 floods
  • Build-breaker + scheduled refresh together cover 100% of containers — active and dormant — with zero ongoing human effort
  • Proves the SRE principle: enforce with automation, not memos
  • Requires SLOs (Tenet 1) and remediation data (Tenet 2) to prioritize which gates to build first
  • Maps to JD: "CI/CD pipelines, container orchestration"
  • Leverages existing GitLab investment and CAB experience
5
Platform Resilience & Capacity
Months 10–15 · Harden
Move from "hope it works" to "prove it works" — chaos engineering, DR testing, capacity forecasting, and AEv3 migration with auto-scaling to avoid missed SLAs during burst processing.
click to expand →

What to Build

  • AEv3 migration with shared capacity pool — move services from AEv2 (Nomad, manual scaling) to AEv3 (Kubernetes, auto-scaling). Establish a shared department overhead pool so any service can burst from normal instance count to MAX_INSTANCES during load spikes, then scale back down. Avoids missed SLAs from hours-long processing delays.
  • Chaos engineering program (game days for Middle Office services)
  • DR/failover testing cadence with measurable RTO/RPO
  • Capacity model per service (normal load, burst ceiling, scaling triggers)

How to Develop

  • Build the business case: document burst processing incidents where 10 instances took hours but 100 could have taken 10 minutes — quantify missed SLAs and client impact
  • Propose shared department overhead pool — all apps contribute to funding the burst capacity, any app can auto-scale into it
  • Pilot AEv3 migration with one high-burst service to prove the auto-scaling model
  • Start chaos with read-only tests: kill one container, measure recovery
  • Graduate to quarterly game days with business stakeholders observing

Why Fifth

  • Directly prevents missed SLAs — burst processing that takes hours today becomes minutes with auto-scaling
  • Requires Tenets 1–4 as inputs (SLOs to define targets, pipelines to deploy, data to build the business case)
  • Chaos engineering is the proof that the practice works
  • Maps to JD: "Kubernetes, fault-tolerant deployments, scalable operations"
6
Culture, Enablement & Scale
Months 15–18+ · Sustain
An SRE practice that lives in one person's head dies when that person leaves. Embed reliability thinking into every team.
click to expand →

What to Build

  • SRE onboarding curriculum (modeled on StanChart's SRE Academy)
  • Embedded SRE coaching for dev teams
  • Reliability review as part of architecture review process
  • Self-service reliability tooling (SLO dashboards, runbook templates)
  • Production readiness checklist for new services

How to Develop

  • Document everything from Tenets 1–5 as playbooks
  • Run monthly "Reliability Office Hours" — open to all dev teams
  • Require production readiness review before any new service goes live
  • Create SRE Champions program: train one engineer per dev team
  • Publish quarterly "State of Reliability" report to leadership

Why Last

  • Culture change requires results to point to — Tenets 1–5 provide them
  • Scales the practice beyond one person
  • Maps to JD: "mentoring engineers, promoting SRE best practices, reliability-first culture"
  • The endgame: SRE as a discipline, not a person
04 — Roadmap

18-Month Execution Plan

Five phases, each producing demonstrable results that justify the next. Early wins land within 5 months; cross-team engineering and organizational change get realistic timelines. Designed so Phase 0 can start Monday.

Phase 0 · Weeks 1–4
Establish the Practice
Deliverables: SRE charter document. Service catalog of Middle Office's top-10 critical services ranked by client impact. Toil baseline (2-week time study). Stakeholder alignment meetings with Dev Leads, COO, Client Services.
Key action: Get explicit agreement that SRE time is protected — not absorbed by BAU incident work. Define the 50/50 rule: max 50% operational work, min 50% engineering.
Phase 1 · Months 2–5
Measure — SLOs, SRE Dashboard, Remediation Pipeline
Deliverables: SLIs/SLOs for top-5 services. Error budget policy (draft). Remediation pipeline added to existing RCA/PRB process. Middle Office SRE Dashboard v1 — built in Grafana, extended into MyCIO, or both — showing SLI gauges, SLO compliance, and error budget burn per service. First weekly SLO burn report to leadership.
Key metric: 100% of P1/P2 PRBs produce tracked remediation action items with ownership and due dates.
Phase 2 · Months 5–10
Automate — Toil Reduction, Pipeline Standards, Release Safety
Deliverables: Standardized GitLab CI pipeline templates with built-in quality gates. Base image compliance system: (1) build-breaker in GitLab CI rejecting stale bases on active builds, (2) scheduled refresh pipeline that auto-rebuilds dormant containers on current bases, opens green MRs or creates remediation JIRAs. Compliance dashboard in MyCIO/Grafana. CI/CD pipeline with automated post-deploy validation. Error-budget-aware release gate (pilot).
Key metric: 30% reduction in toil hours. Change failure rate tracked and trending down. Base image compliance back to >95% across all containers (active and dormant).
Phase 3 · Months 10–15
Harden — Resilience, Capacity, Platform
Deliverables: AEv3 business case with quantified SLA impact from burst processing delays. Shared capacity pool proposal with funding model. AEv3 pilot migration for one high-burst service. First chaos engineering game day completed. DR playbook with measured RTO/RPO.
Key metric: Mean Time to Recovery (MTTR) reduced by 40% from baseline. Pilot service burst processing: hours → minutes.
Phase 4 · Months 15–18
Scale — Culture, Training, Self-Service
Deliverables: SRE onboarding curriculum published. Production readiness checklist enforced for new services. SRE Champions identified in 2+ dev teams. "State of Reliability" Q1 report to department leadership.
Key metric: At least 2 dev teams self-managing their own SLO dashboards and remediation tracking.
